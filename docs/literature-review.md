**Document type:** structured literature-review scaffold  
**Status:** working document; not yet a systematic review  
**Scope:** altered gravity research, artificial gravity, centrifugation, analogues, space biology, plant science, and research-infrastructure context  
**Last updated:** 2026-06-24

---

## 1. Purpose

This document provides the first structured literature-review scaffold for the Hypergravity Habitat project. It is intended to support academic discussion, expert review, and proposal preparation.

It is not yet a complete systematic review. Its purpose is to define the review domains, identify institutional source anchors, record early relevance judgments, and make explicit which claims still require peer-reviewed support.

The review question is:

> Does the existing altered-gravity research landscape leave a meaningful gap for sustained moderate hypergravity as a controlled terrestrial research environment?

---

## 2. Review Position

The current project position is cautious:

1. Microgravity, bed rest, isolation, parabolic flight, human centrifugation, and biological centrifugation are well-established research domains.
2. Artificial gravity has been studied through models, centrifuges, bed-rest countermeasure studies, and rotating-system concepts.
3. Biological hypergravity studies exist, especially for cells, plants, microorganisms, and animal models.
4. What appears less established is a habitat-scale or laboratory-scale environment dedicated to sustained moderate hypergravity above 1 g for long enough to study adaptation rather than only acute response.
5. This apparent gap must be verified through systematic review before it is used as the basis of a funding proposal.

## 4. Institutional Source Anchors

### 4.1 NASA Human Research Program

NASA describes its Human Research Program as using research to protect astronaut health and performance, including ground research facilities, the International Space Station, and analogue environments.

Project relevance:

- establishes the importance of health and performance research for exploration missions,
- supports the use of ground-based and analogue platforms,
- provides context for countermeasure research,
- provides terminology for risk, performance, and mission relevance.

Source:

- NASA Human Research Program: https://www.nasa.gov/hrp/

Project implication:

A Hypergravity Habitat should be framed as a possible analogue or pre-feasibility research platform, not as a substitute for existing NASA HRP platforms.

---

### 4.2 DLR :envihab

DLR describes :envihab in Cologne as a medical research facility of the DLR Institute of Aerospace Medicine where environmental effects on humans and countermeasures are studied. The facility includes modules relevant to altered-gravity and aerospace-medicine research, including the DLR Short-Arm Centrifuge, living and simulation areas, PET-MRI, psychology laboratory, and biology laboratory.

Project relevance:

- demonstrates that aerospace medicine uses sophisticated terrestrial research infrastructure,
- shows that human health, performance, countermeasures, bed rest, isolation, lighting, and immobilization are already studied in integrated environments,
- provides a strong comparator for any Hypergravity Habitat proposal.

Source:

- DLR :envihab: https://www.dlr.de/en/envihab

Project implication:

The Hypergravity Habitat concept should be benchmarked against :envihab-style facilities. The project must explain what sustained moderate hypergravity would add beyond existing centrifuge, bed-rest, isolation, and environmental-research capabilities.

---

### 4.3 NASA Biological and Physical Sciences

NASA Biological and Physical Sciences describes research that uses space stressors such as radiation and microgravity to study biological and physical phenomena for exploration and benefits on Earth. NASA’s Space Biology Program studies how gravity affects living organisms and how biological systems accommodate to spaceflight environments.

Project relevance:

- establishes gravity as a valid biological variable,
- supports the inclusion of plants, microorganisms, cells, tissues, and physical-sciences payloads,
- provides a context for space crops, space laboratories, and biological response to altered environments.

Source:

- NASA Biological & Physical Sciences: https://science.nasa.gov/biological-physical/

Project implication:

Biological payloads are a strong early path for the project because they align with established space-biology questions while avoiding early human-subject risk.

---

### 4.4 ESA Research and ISS Facilities

ESA provides European context for human and robotic exploration research, ISS research facilities, bed-rest studies, and biological payloads. ESA’s Columbus laboratory and related facility ecosystem should be reviewed in detail in later versions of this document.

Source anchors to verify and expand:

- ESA Human and Robotic Exploration Research: https://www.esa.int/Science_Exploration/Human_and_Robotic_Exploration/Research
- ESA Biolab and ISS research facility documentation: to be expanded with stable official URLs and facility manuals.
- ESA Large Diameter Centrifuge / ESTEC documentation: to be expanded with official facility specifications and peer-reviewed descriptions.

Project implication:

ESA-related facilities may already cover important parts of biological hypergravity and microgravity research. The project must distinguish sustained moderate hypergravity from existing biological centrifuge and ISS payload capabilities.

## 5. Review Domains

### 5.1 Human Spaceflight Physiology

Relevant topics:

- musculoskeletal deconditioning,
- bone loss,
- cardiovascular deconditioning,
- vestibular and sensorimotor adaptation,
- fluid shifts,
- sleep and circadian disruption,
- exercise countermeasures,
- post-flight readaptation,
- astronaut health risk management.

Key review questions:

- Which effects are caused primarily by unloading?
- Which countermeasures are currently used?
- Which questions remain unresolved for partial gravity and artificial gravity?
- How could moderate hypergravity complement current knowledge?

Status:

- strong existing field,
- requires systematic peer-reviewed literature review,
- should be linked to NASA HRP risk categories and DLR aerospace medicine research.

---

### 5.2 Bed-Rest and Immobilization Analogues

Relevant topics:

- head-down tilt bed rest,
- immobilization,
- unloading models,
- countermeasure studies,
- artificial-gravity countermeasure trials,
- nutrition and exercise protocols,
- long-duration medical monitoring.

Key review questions:

- Which physiological changes are reliably induced by bed rest?
- Which countermeasures have been tested?
- How have short-arm centrifuges been used in bed-rest protocols?
- What are the limitations of bed rest as an analogue for altered gravity?

Project implication:

Bed-rest studies provide a strong methodological model for controlled human research, but they do not create sustained elevated effective gravity.

---

### 5.3 Artificial Gravity and Human Centrifugation

Relevant topics:

- short-arm centrifugation,
- long-arm centrifugation,
- intermittent artificial gravity,
- centrifuge tolerance,
- vestibular side effects,
- Coriolis effects,
- artificial gravity as a countermeasure,
- rotating spacecraft concepts.

Key review questions:

- What exposure durations have been tested?
- Which gravity levels and body-axis gradients have been used?
- What side effects limit human tolerance?
- How does intermittent exposure compare with sustained exposure?
- What empirical evidence exists for artificial gravity as a countermeasure?

Project implication:

The Hypergravity Habitat project should position itself as complementary to artificial-gravity centrifuge research. It should avoid claiming novelty merely from using centrifugal acceleration; the possible novelty is sustained, controlled, moderate hypergravity as a broader environment.

---

### 5.4 Biological Hypergravity

Relevant topics:

- cell culture under hypergravity,
- microbial growth,
- biofilm formation,
- cytoskeleton and mechanotransduction,
- tissue models,
- simple organisms,
- animal models,
- multi-generation studies.

Key review questions:

- Which model systems respond reliably to hypergravity?
- What gravity levels and durations have been used?
- Which effects are direct gravity responses and which are confounded by vibration, sedimentation, shear, or handling?
- Which systems are suitable for low-risk early demonstrators?

Project implication:

Biological payloads may be the best early experimental route. However, biological interpretation requires strong environmental and vibration controls.

---

### 5.5 Plant Science and Gravitropism

Relevant topics:

- root and shoot gravitropism,
- statolith and auxin-mediated gravity sensing,
- seedling development,
- root architecture,
- water and nutrient transport,
- photosynthesis,
- biomass allocation,
- flowering and reproduction,
- multi-generation plant response.

Key review questions:

- Which plants are most sensitive to modest gravity changes?
- What levels above 1 g have been studied?
- Which endpoints are most reproducible?
- How do light, irrigation, humidity, substrate, and vibration confound results?
- Could plant payloads provide the first useful demonstrator?

Project implication:

Plant studies are a high-priority early science candidate because they are measurable, scalable, ethically simpler than animal or human work, and relevant to space agriculture.

---

### 5.6 Space Biology and Open Data

Relevant topics:

- NASA Open Science Data Repository,
- NASA Life Sciences Portal,
- spaceflight and ground-analogue datasets,
- plant, animal, cell, and microbial space-biology data,
- metadata standards,
- reproducibility.

Key review questions:

- Which existing datasets can inform Hypergravity Habitat hypotheses?
- Are there datasets comparing 1 g, microgravity, partial gravity, and hypergravity?
- What metadata standards should the project adopt?

Project implication:

The project should align future data products with open-science practices and capture complete environmental metadata.

---

### 5.7 Engineering and Ride Quality

Relevant topics:

- rail vibration,
- maglev vibration,
- rotating-platform dynamics,
- human comfort under lateral acceleration,
- emergency braking,
- continuous operation,
- maintenance and wear,
- environmental control in moving laboratories,
- payload interface standards.

Key review questions:

- What vibration levels are acceptable for biological and human experiments?
- Can a railway platform provide sufficient measurement quality?
- Would maglev materially improve experimental quality?
- What emergency and transfer systems are credible?

Project implication:

The engineering literature must be reviewed not only for construction feasibility but for experimental validity.

## 6. Research-Gap Test

The literature review should test the project premise against the following questions.

| Test | Question | Possible outcome |

| Facility overlap | Do existing facilities already provide sustained moderate hypergravity? | revise or reject gap |
| Duration | Have long-duration moderate hypergravity studies already been done? | narrow gap |
| Human exposure | Are human data limited to acute or intermittent exposure? | supports gap if verified |
| Biology | Are biological hypergravity studies sufficient for early payload design? | supports demonstrator path |
| Engineering | Are radius, speed, vibration, and safety constraints prohibitive? | may redirect architecture |
| Funding fit | Does the topic align with real institutional priorities? | supports or weakens proposal |

## 9. Inclusion Criteria for Formal Review

A later systematic review should include sources that:

- report gravity level or acceleration conditions,
- report exposure duration,
- describe experimental controls,
- include biological, physiological, human-factors, or engineering endpoints,
- provide enough methodological detail to compare relevance,
- distinguish acute, intermittent, and sustained exposure.

---

## 10. Exclusion Criteria for Formal Review

Exclude or treat separately:

- speculative architecture concepts without empirical or engineering analysis,
- popular articles unless used only as background,
- studies without clear acceleration conditions,
- studies where gravity cannot be separated from vibration or other confounders,
- high-g military or aviation studies when not relevant to moderate sustained exposure,
- microgravity-only studies unless they support contrast or countermeasure logic.

## 12. Preliminary Interpretation

The institutional sources support the general relevance of altered-gravity research, terrestrial analogues, human health and performance, countermeasures, and space biology. They do not by themselves prove that a Hypergravity Habitat is needed.

The project’s gap remains plausible but unproven:

- Existing infrastructure clearly covers microgravity, bed rest, isolation, short-arm centrifugation, and biological payloads.
- The project must demonstrate that sustained moderate hypergravity as a continuous, controlled, habitat-scale or payload-scale environment is not already adequately covered.
- The first credible experimental path is likely not human habitation, but an instrumented biological or physical payload demonstrator.
